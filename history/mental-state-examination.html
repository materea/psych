    Introduction

    Very recent symptoms and signs are often recorded in the MSE, even if the phenomena are not experienced or elicited during the interview.

    Behaviour and Appearance

    • Describe Dressing (appropriateness, cleanliness)
    • Grooming (hair, nails, body odour)
    • Behaviour:
      • Acts too young or older than age
      • Behaves like the opposite sex
      • Seductive inappropriate behaviour
      • Overly dependent, manipulative
      • Overly dramatic
      • Disinhibited
      • Attitude towards the interview (unmotivated, level of cooperation, shy, withdrawn, reluctant to talk)
    • Psychomotor Activity:
      • Normal, reduced or increased
      • Restless, agitated
      • Abnormal movement or posture
      • Fidgeting
      • Repetitive Behaviours (compulsions, rocking, hand flapping)
      • Habits (nail biting, hair pulling, nose picking)
    • Maintenance of Eye Contact

    Affect and Mood

    Mood/Feelings (Self-report)

    Ask how the individual has been feeling on most days in the last two weeks.

    E.g., unhappy, sad, depressed, worried, apathetic, happy, angry, nervous, anxious, guilty.

    Affect (Observed)

    Observe the individual‚Äôs response to different environmental stimuli and cues for appropriate or inappropriate affect. The affect could be reactive, angry, irritable, anxious, elated, labile (sudden changes in mood), perplexed, suspicious, blunt, flat.

    Perception

    Perception is the conscious awareness of elements in the environment by the mental processing of sensory stimuli

    Hallucination

    False sensory perception occurring in the absence of any relevant external stimulation of the sensory modality involved

    Perception in a particular sensory modality in the absence of a real external stimulus; present in external objective space, not subject to termination by effort or will - auditory, visual, gustatory, tactile, olfactory, or kinesthetic.

    Illusion

    Perceptual misinterpretation of a real external stimulus.

    Misinterpretation of a normal perception ‚Äì affect, pareidolic, or completion illusion.

    Other phenomena include:

    • Imagery
    • Derealization
    • Depersonalization
    • Thought Echo

    Talk (Speech) & Thoughts

    Talk/Speech

    The domains assessed are:

    • Spontaneity = willingness and readiness to speak
    • Coherence = clarity of spoken words
    • Relevance = appropriateness to questions asked/topic of discussion
    • Tone = pitch of voice (high, low, or normal)
    • Volume = quantity of words spoken (increased, normal, or reduced)

    Thought Process

    The domains assessed are:

    • Stream (speed or rapidity of flow of speech): increased, fast, or pressured; normal or free-flowing, slow or reduced
    • Form (degree of connectivity of patient‚Äôs ideas; is it logical?): connected themes, flight of ideas (with use of puns, rhymes, and clang associations), loosening of associations (derailment, tangentiality, knight‚Äôs move thinking), circumstantiality or over-inclusion, neologisms, perseveration
    • Content: normal or abnormal (ideas, delusions, obsessions, preoccupations, overvalued ideas, phobias)
      • Delusion

        • False belief, based on incorrect inference about external reality, that is firmly held despite objective and obvious contradictory proof or evidence and despite the fact that other members of the culture do not share the belief.

        • Delusion of Control
        • Delusion of Grandeur
        • Delusion of Infidelity
        • Delusion of Persecution
        • Delusion of Reference
        • Somatic Delusion
        • Nihilistic Delusion
      • Preoccupation of Thought

        • Centering of thought content on a particular idea, associated with a strong affective tone, such as a paranoid trend or a suicidal or homicidal preoccupation

      • Overvalued Idea

        • False or unreasonable belief or idea that is sustained beyond the bounds of reason. It is held with less intensity or duration than a delusion, but is usually associated with mental illness

      • Obsession

        • Persistent and recurrent idea, image, or impulse that cannot be eliminated from consciousness by logic or reasoning

      • Compulsion

        • Pathological need to act on an impulse that, if resisted, produces anxiety; repetitive behavior in response to an obsession or performed according to certain rules, with no true end in itself other than to prevent something from occurring in the future.

    • Possession (experience of patient's thought remaining private) - thought broadcast, thought insertion, thought withdrawal
      • Thought Insertion

        • Delusion that thoughts are being implanted in one's mind by other people or forces.

      • Thought Withdrawal

        • Delusion that one's thoughts are being removed from one's mind by other people or forces.

      • Thought Broadcasting

        • Delusion that one's thoughts are being broadcast or projected into the environment.

      • Suicidal Ideation
      • Homicidal Ideations

    Orientation

    Cognitive Functions - Level of Awareness

    Write tests and answers in the patient‚Äôs sheet.

    Orientation in Time: Ask day, date, month, season, and year

    Place: Ask about floor, town, city, state, country

    Person: Ask the patient to identify familiar persons

    Attention and Concentration

    Attention is the ability to focus on a subject at hand; Concentration is the ability to maintain that focus; the problems may be lapses in attention or being easily distracted.

    Assess with any of the tests below:

    Serial 7 Subtraction Test:

    Subtract 7 from 100 and keep subtracting 7 from each answer until you get a number less than 7. Cite an example for the patient with serial subtraction of 3 from 20.

    The Serial 7 test is timed. A patient can fail in number (fails > 2 subtractions) or fail in time (gets the answers but overshoots the time; time for completion of the test is 60-90 seconds).

    Mark dots per second with the answers provided by the patient on the patient‚Äôs sheet as the test is ongoing, indicating how long it takes the patient to solve each step of the subtraction (e.g., 93..86..79.72..65.58). It took one second for the patient to solve 100 - 7 = 93; the time spent so far in the example is 15 seconds, adding the dots and the answers provided.

    Memory: Immediate, Recent, and Remote Memory

    Registration and Immediate Recall: Simple name and address (consisting of a name and address novel to the patient) or show 3 dissimilar items and ask the patient to repeat immediately after you.

    Recent or Short-term Memory Testing:

    • 5-minute Name and Address Test: ‚ÄúJames Sama, 5 Logan Street, Koko‚Äù ‚Äì the sentence should contain 5 ‚Äì 7 bytes (words) of information, just as the capacity of short-term memory, to avoid substitution. The patient should not fail > 3 of the information. Use novel words to remove bias of recalling from long-term memory. Recall after 5 minutes.
    • Repeat 3 Dissimilar Items 5 Minutes After They Have Been Presented
    • Babcock Sentence Test (e.g., ‚ÄúOne thing a nation must have to be rich and great is a large supply of wood‚Äù) ‚Äì Recall after 5 minutes

    Long-term Memory Testing: Events in the patient‚Äôs past that can be corroborated; past important national events and dates.

    Intellect

    ‚Äì Ability to Generalize Concepts

    Tested by:

    • Abstraction: Interpretation of Common Proverbs (e.g., ‚Äòmake hay while the sun shines‚Äô)
    • Calculation ability: Ask the patient to solve simple arithmetic questions
    • General Fund of Knowledge: Ask about events, places, or activities they are expected to know
    • Similarities and Differences Between 3 Named Objects

    Judgment

    Ability or capacity to make the appropriate decisions and act on them in social situations.

    Subjective

    Assessed by asking what the patient would do in certain hypothetical social situations and why, e.g., the running water test.

    Objective

    Inferred from the patient‚Äôs actions and decisions during the interview.

    Insight

    Awareness of morbid change in oneself and a correct attitude to this. Assessed by asking if the patient:

    • Recognizes certain phenomena in self that others consider abnormal
    • Attributes these abnormalities to a mental illness
    • Realizes that treatment is required
    • Is willing to accept treatment if offered
    • Appreciates the consequences of the illness, e.g., associated disability, concerns of relatives, and need for involuntary treatment
